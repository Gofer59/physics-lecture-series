# LQCD-V05 — Numerical Gauge Generation and Dynamical Fermions | Narration Transcript

## Scene 01

A realistic lattice has on the order of ten million link variables. Each is a three by three SU(3) matrix. The partition function is an integral over all of them, weighted by e to the minus S.

Ten million links. Direct integration would finish approximately after the heat death of the universe.

So we do not integrate. We sample. Generate a Markov chain of configurations distributed according to e to the minus S over Z, then average observables over the chain. Monte Carlo turns an impossible integral into a tractable simulation.

## Scene 02

A Markov chain proposes a new configuration U prime from the current U with some transition probability. Detailed balance requires the forward step times the equilibrium weight of U to equal the reverse step times the weight of U prime.

Ergodicity requires every configuration to be eventually reachable. Together the two conditions guarantee that the chain samples the correct distribution.

Every lattice algorithm you will ever meet is just some clever way of satisfying these two rules.

## Scene 03

For pure gauge theory the Metropolis algorithm is very direct. Pick a link. Propose a multiplicative update — U goes to X times U, with X near the identity. Compute the change in action. Only the four staples touching that link contribute, so it is cheap.

Accept with probability minimum of one and e to the minus delta S. Reject means you leave U alone. Sweep through every link, then repeat.

Simple, exact, and fully local. This is how lattice simulations started in 1980.

## Scene 04

Heat bath goes further. It replaces a link with a sample drawn directly from its conditional distribution given the neighbors. No rejection, full thermalization at each link. For SU(2) there are exact methods; for SU(3) we embed three SU(2) subgroups, the Cabibbo–Marinari trick.

Overrelaxation makes a deterministic move that preserves the action. It explores the constant-action surface without changing the weight.

Mix three to five overrelaxations per heat bath step. Autocorrelations drop dramatically.

## Scene 05

Start from a cold lattice, with all links equal to the identity, or from a hot one with random SU(3) matrices. The first few thousand sweeps are thermalization. Throw them away.

Once equilibrated, successive configurations are still correlated. The integrated autocorrelation time, tau-int, tells you how many sweeps separate effectively independent samples. Good observables have tau-int around ten. Some, like the topological charge at fine lattice spacing, have tau-int of thousands.

Topological freezing: when your Monte Carlo chain would really just prefer not to.

## Scene 06

Naive error bars on correlated samples are wrong. They understate the uncertainty by a factor of square root of two tau-int. That is not a rounding issue.

The standard fix is binning — average over bin sizes much larger than tau-int until the error stabilizes. Jackknife leaves one out at a time. Bootstrap resamples with replacement.

Every modern lattice paper reports one of these with explicit autocorrelation correction. Getting the error bar right matters as much as getting the mean.

## Scene 07

Now add quarks. The partition function picks up a factor of determinant of D of U to the N sub f. This determinant is a functional of the gauge field. Every gauge update changes it.

Direct computation scales like the volume cubed. For a realistic lattice that is completely impossible.

For years the workaround was the quenched approximation: set the determinant to a constant. It costs about ten percent accuracy.

Quenched QCD: a perfectly good approximation, if you are willing to delete the sea quarks from reality.

## Scene 08

The trick is to write determinant of D-dagger D as a Gaussian integral over an auxiliary bosonic field phi. The integrand becomes e to the minus phi-dagger times the inverse of D-dagger D times phi.

The fermionic degrees of freedom are gone. The path integral is now purely bosonic, with an effective action we can sample.

For two degenerate flavors this gives the determinant squared exactly. For one flavor, or for rooted staggered fermions, we reach for a rational approximation. The determinant never appears directly. Only solver calls do.

## Scene 09

Hybrid Monte Carlo — Duane, Kennedy, Pendleton, and Roweth, 1987. Give every link a fictitious momentum, drawn from a Gaussian. Evolve the links and momenta together under Hamilton's equations for a molecular dynamics trajectory.

At the end of the trajectory, do a Metropolis accept or reject on the total fictitious energy, delta H.

If the integrator were perfect, acceptance would be one hundred percent. Because it is discrete there are small delta H fluctuations, and Metropolis corrects them exactly. A global update that changes the whole lattice at once.

## Scene 10

We solve Hamilton's equations by leapfrog. Half-step the momenta, full-step the links, half-step the momenta again. It is symplectic, preserving phase-space volume, and time-reversible.

The Hamiltonian error per step is order epsilon squared. Higher-order integrators like Omelyan reach order epsilon to the fourth, at the cost of more force evaluations per step.

The whole of HMC rests on the integrator being time-reversible and symplectic. Drop either one and detailed balance fails.

## Scene 11

The molecular dynamics force has two pieces. The gauge force is cheap — a sum of staples, directly computable. The fermion force is not.

To evaluate the fermion force you must solve D-dagger D chi equals phi for the pseudofermion field. That is a Krylov solver call, every single force evaluation.

At light quark masses the solver becomes expensive. Its cost scales like one over the quark mass squared. Solver time dominates the simulation budget.

## Scene 12

For an odd number of flavors, or for rooted staggered simulations, we need determinant of D-dagger D raised to a fractional power. Rational Hybrid Monte Carlo handles this.

Factor the fractional power into a sum of partial fractions. Each one becomes a separate pseudofermion with a shifted inverse. The Remez algorithm tunes the coefficients to machine precision.

RHMC is exact up to the quality of the rational approximation. MILC, HPQCD, and several others use it routinely.

## Scene 13

A handful of algorithmic tricks turn impossible simulations into routine ones. Multiple time scales, from Sexton and Weingarten — finer steps for the expensive fermion force, coarser for the cheap gauge force.

Mass preconditioning, due to Hasenbusch, splits the determinant into products indexed by heavier auxiliary masses, smoothing the spectrum. Even-odd preconditioning exploits a checkerboard structure and halves the linear system.

Together these give an order-of-magnitude speedup. They are the difference between "cannot simulate the physical pion" and "done routinely."

## Scene 14

The innermost loop is always a Krylov solver for D x equals b. Conjugate gradient applies to D-dagger D, where gamma-five hermiticity helps. BiCGStab and GMRES handle non-Hermitian D directly.

For near-chiral quark masses, multigrid solvers give ten to a hundred times speedups by mixing length scales. Domain-decomposition preconditioners accelerate things further.

The solver determines the wall-clock cost of everything. Lattice QCD is secretly just ten years of solver engineering with some physics layered on top.

## Scene 15

A decade ago, simulating at the physical pion mass of one hundred thirty-five MeV was a heroic accomplishment. Today it is the baseline.

Better algorithms, better solvers, and faster hardware have pushed the chiral extrapolation out of the picture for many observables. You just simulate at the physical point.

This is one of the quiet revolutions of the field. Nearly invisible from outside, transformative inside.

## Scene 16

Push toward zero lattice spacing and the topological charge Q-top gets stuck. Monte Carlo sweeps can no longer tunnel between topological sectors. The barriers between them grow as a shrinks.

For lattice spacings below about zero point zero five femtometers, topology freezes on simulation timescales.

Workarounds include open boundary conditions in time, tempered boundary conditions, and machine-learning-assisted sampling. This is an active research area, not a solved one.

The lattice, having discovered topology, refuses to change its mind.

## Scene 17

A typical modern ensemble: a sixty-four cubed by one hundred twenty-eight lattice, physical pion mass, domain-wall or clover fermions, several thousand configurations.

Cost: roughly ten million core-hours. Storage: terabytes. One ensemble is a piece of infrastructure.

Ensembles are shared across collaborations. One group generates, many measure. International consortia like CLS and MILC produce libraries that dozens of physics projects rely on.

## Scene 18

Modern efforts apply normalizing flows, score-based models, and neural-network proposals to help HMC sample more efficiently. The targets are topological freezing and near-chiral simulations.

Most methods are still in proof-of-concept for full QCD. For toy models like the Schwinger model or phi to the fourth, they already beat traditional sampling.

Machine learning for lattice QCD is a rapidly moving frontier.

The lattice, meet the neural network. Be nice to each other.

## Scene 19

Lattice gauge configurations are generated by Markov chain Monte Carlo. For pure gauge theory we use Metropolis, heat bath, and overrelaxation.

For dynamical fermions, the pseudofermion trick converts the determinant into a bosonic action. Hybrid Monte Carlo evolves that action by molecular dynamics plus a Metropolis accept-reject.

Modern tricks — multi-step integration, mass preconditioning, multigrid solvers — make physical pion mass simulations routine. Autocorrelation and topological freezing remain the open problems.

## Scene 20

We can generate configurations. Now we need to decide what to measure.

Next time: two-point correlators, the effective mass plateau, smearing to improve signal, the variational method for excited states, and correlated multi-state fits.

Then scale setting via the Sommer parameter or Wilson flow, and the continuum and chiral extrapolations that turn dimensionless lattice numbers into physical predictions in MeV.
