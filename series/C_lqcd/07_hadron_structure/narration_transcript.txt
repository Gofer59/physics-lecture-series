# LQCD-V07 — Hadron Structure and Finite Volume Effects | Narration Transcript

## Scene 01

Two-point functions give hadron masses. For precision flavor physics — CKM elements, B-meson mixing, meson decays — we need matrix elements of operators between hadron states. That requires three-point functions.

For internal structure — how a proton's momentum and spin are shared between quarks and gluons — we need parton distributions.

Lattice QCD goes beyond the spectrum when it starts answering the real question: what is a proton made of?

Masses were the easy part. Now we ask the proton to open up and talk about its feelings.

## Scene 02

A three-point function has three insertions. Create a hadron at time zero. Insert a current J at time t. Annihilate the hadron at time t sub s.

At large source-sink separation, the ground state dominates on both sides. The three-point function factorizes: mod Z zero squared times e to the minus m sub H times t sub s, multiplied by the matrix element of the current between ground-state hadrons.

Divide by the two-point function and the exponential prefactors cancel. The desired matrix element pops out.

## Scene 03

The pion decay constant f-pi is defined by the matrix element of the axial current between the pion and the vacuum. The vacuum-to-pion amplitude is i f-pi times the pion momentum.

On the lattice, extract f-pi from a two-point function of axial currents.

Similarly, f-K fixes the ratio V-us over V-ud. The B-meson decay constants, f sub B and f sub B s, control B-meson mixing and the extraction of V-ub and V-td. Modern lattice calculations quote these at sub-percent precision.

## Scene 04

For a decay like B to pi ell nu, the hadronic matrix element factorizes into vector and scalar form factors, f-plus and f-zero of q squared.

The decay rate equals mod V-ub squared times an integral of mod f-plus squared. The lattice computes the form factors; experiment measures the rate. Combining them gives the CKM element.

Lattice QCD is the single dominant source of hadronic uncertainty in most CKM extractions. A tension currently exists between inclusive and exclusive V-cb — the lattice is central to resolving it.

## Scene 05

Neutral kaon mixing is governed by a four-quark operator with delta-S equals two. The bag parameter B sub K parametrizes its matrix element between K-bar and K.

B sub K enters the epsilon-K constraint in the unitarity triangle.

Computing it requires non-perturbative renormalization of a four-quark operator — a demanding technical problem. The FLAG world average, B-K-hat equals zero point seven six two five with an error of ninety-seven in the last digits, is a flagship lattice result.

## Scene 06

Parton distribution functions are defined through matrix elements of field operators separated along the light cone. The light cone is a feature of Minkowski spacetime. On a Euclidean lattice, the light cone collapses to a point.

You cannot directly compute a PDF on a Euclidean lattice. This is not a technical inconvenience. It is a structural feature of the formalism.

For decades the lattice could compute only the lowest few Mellin moments, and only up to the third moment before hypercubic-group operator mixing became uncontrollable.

The lattice's oldest unspoken problem: we went Euclidean for the path integral, and the light cone refused to come with us.

## Scene 07

In 2013 Ji proposed the large-momentum effective theory. Compute matrix elements with purely spatial operator separations on a nucleon boosted to large momentum along z, then match perturbatively to the light-cone PDF.

In 2017 Radyushkin refined the idea into the pseudo-distribution approach, using the Ioffe time, nu equals p times z, as the natural variable.

Both methods finally give the lattice access to x-dependent parton distributions. The old inverse problem of reconstructing PDFs from discrete moments is replaced by a direct calculation.

## Scene 08

The reduced pseudo-Ioffe-time distribution M of nu and z-squared, computed on the lattice, is matched to the MS-bar light-cone PDF through a kernel. Schematically: M equals the integral of K against Q, plus corrections of order z-squared times Lambda-QCD squared.

The kernel is known in perturbation theory and includes the Altarelli–Parisi splitting functions at next-to-leading order.

Lattice results for the nucleon isovector momentum fraction agree with phenomenological fits from CT18 and NNPDF, within uncertainties.

## Scene 09

Generalized parton distributions extend PDFs to include momentum transfer between initial and final states. They depend on three variables: x, the longitudinal momentum fraction; xi, the skewness; and t, the total momentum transfer squared.

At xi equals zero the Fourier transform in t gives a two-dimensional impact-parameter density. That is literally the quark distribution projected onto the transverse plane.

GPDs are nucleon tomography.

## Scene 10

Ji's sum rule says that twice the total angular momentum carried by quarks of flavor q equals the integral over x of x times H-q plus E-q, evaluated at zero skewness and zero t.

Sum over flavors, add the gluons, and you must recover one-half.

This is a direct route to the proton spin puzzle. The first moment of H is the momentum fraction; the integral involving E gives orbital angular momentum. The lattice can compute all ingredients.

The proton spin: thirty percent quarks, some gluons, a surprising amount of orbital angular momentum, and an ongoing disagreement about the remaining percent.

## Scene 11

Three-point functions are dominated by excited-state contamination much more severely than two-point functions. At the physical pion mass, the nucleon's first excited state contaminates down to very late source-sink separations.

Strategies: compute at several sink times and extrapolate to infinity. Perform explicit two-state fits. Or use the summation method, which sums the ratio over the insertion time and extracts the matrix element from the slope.

This is typically the dominant systematic in nucleon structure calculations.

## Scene 12

Your simulation lives in a box — L cubed times T. Pions, the lightest hadrons, feel the box most strongly. Their Compton wavelength is the largest.

The rule of thumb: keep m-pi times L above four, and finite-volume corrections stay below about one percent.

Chiral perturbation theory predicts the leading correction falls like e to the minus m-pi L, divided by m-pi L to the three-halves. Smaller boxes can be pushed into the chiral regime, where finite volume is a feature, not a bug.

## Scene 13

Push the box small enough that m-pi L is of order one. Now zero modes of the Dirac operator dominate the path integral. The theory reduces to a chiral random matrix theory, in which low-energy constants become measurable directly.

The Banks–Casher relation holds volume by volume.

The epsilon regime is a controlled corner of QCD, where finite-volume physics is calculable in closed form. It is a probe, not a problem.

## Scene 14

Two interacting particles in a finite box do not have free momenta. Their interaction modifies the allowed energy levels.

Luscher, in 1986, showed exactly how. The finite-volume spectrum determines the infinite-volume scattering phase shift through a condition: delta-zero of k plus phi of q equals n times pi. The variable q is k L over two pi, and phi is a known geometric function.

The box is no longer a contaminant. It is an interferometer. Extracting scattering phase shifts becomes a lattice calculation.

Finite volume: the bug that became the feature.

## Scene 15

A resonance like the rho meson is a short-lived unstable state — not available as an asymptotic state on a lattice.

But through the Luscher method, you can extract the pi-pi scattering phase shift, identify where it passes through pi over two, and read off the rho mass and width.

Modern extensions handle multiple channels — the sigma, the K-star, the f-zero — moving frames for more kinematic points, and coupled channels like pi-pi mixing with K K-bar. Resonance spectroscopy is now routine lattice physics.

## Scene 16

On the lattice you have a choice. Use a point-split conserved current — exactly conserved but non-local. Or use a local current, which requires a renormalization factor Z sub V.

Ward identities relate different renormalization constants and provide non-trivial consistency checks. Partial conservation of the axial current holds on the lattice up to order-a corrections in the massless limit.

These identities constrain the systematic errors in precision matrix elements.

## Scene 17

The precision deliverables of modern lattice QCD: quark masses for u, d, s, c, and b at sub-percent errors. Decay constants — f-pi, f-K, f-B, f-Bs — at sub-percent. Form factors f-plus for K to pi and B to pi at a few percent. Bag parameters B-K and B-B at a few percent.

These numbers feed into every Standard Model test — CKM unitarity, K and B mixing, rare meson decays, direct CP violation.

Lattice QCD is, quietly, a pillar of beyond-Standard-Model searches.

## Scene 18

Not everything is solved. Isospin-breaking effects and electromagnetic corrections require dynamical QED — now being simulated but expensive.

Long-distance contributions to K to pi pi require handling multi-hadron final states through Luscher methods. Gluon PDFs are notoriously hard because of operator mixing.

The strong CP problem and theta-vacuum physics need better control of topological sampling. Transverse-momentum-dependent PDFs are a frontier. The Electron-Ion Collider era will demand more.

## Scene 19

Three-point functions give matrix elements, decay constants, form factors, and CKM elements.

The light-cone obstruction blocks direct PDFs on Euclidean lattices. Quasi-PDFs and pseudo-distributions solve this through perturbative matching.

Generalized parton distributions encode nucleon tomography through the Ji sum rule. Excited-state contamination is the dominant systematic in hadron structure. Finite-volume corrections are exponentially small for m-pi L above four. And the Luscher method turns finite volume into a tool for scattering and resonances.
